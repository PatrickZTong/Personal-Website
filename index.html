About

My research combines communication theory and computational methods to examine political rhetoric, digital technologies, and social inequality. I focus on interpretable empirical designs that bridge substantive communication questions with robust quantitative evidence.

I completed a BA in Journalism and Communication at Macau University of Science and Technology (GPA 3.77/4.00, Top 1%), and I am currently pursuing graduate training in machine learning, natural language processing, social network analysis, and social research methods.

Research

Accepted Conference Papers

Accepted · IAMCR 2026 (Political Communication Research Section)

Exclusionary Populism in the U.S.: From Party-wide Patterns to Trump’s Rhetoric

Galway, Ireland · Jun–Jul 2026

Built a party–month panel from U.S. presidential campaign speeches (1952–2024) using the Annenberg Presidential Database and LIWC measures to examine exclusionary populist rhetoric over time.

Methods: LIWC text measures, longitudinal modeling, reproducible Python workflow, visualization.

Accepted · IAMCR 2026 (Ethics of Society and Ethics of Communication WG)

When the Dead Seem Present: Perceived Realism, Social Presence, and Psychological Mechanisms in AI-based Digital Resurrection

Galway, Ireland · Jun–Jul 2026

Developed an ethics-centered framework and preregistered online survey experiment to test how perceived realism and social presence shape intention toward AI-based digital resurrection.

Methods: Between-subjects experiment, moderated parallel mediation, robustness checks.

Submitted Papers

Submitted · NCA 2026 (Political Communication Division)

Speaking as the People, the Protector, or the Provocateur: Rhetorical Persona Types in U.S. Presidential Campaign Speeches (1952–2024)

Investigates whether candidates adopt recurring rhetorical personae rather than occupying a single populism continuum.

Methods: LIWC lexical indicators, latent profile analysis, comparative historical analysis.

Submitted · NCA 2026 (Family Communication Division)

When Home Moves Away: The Causal Effect of Parental Rural-to-Urban Migration on Rural-Origin Children’s Intergenerational Ties in China

Examines how migration arrangements (left-behind, co-migrant, non-migrant) influence communication and relationship outcomes among rural-origin children.

Methods: CFPS panel data, propensity-score matching (PSM), difference-in-differences (DiD).
